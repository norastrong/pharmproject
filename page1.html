How do you become a pharmacist?

An important part of deciding a on a career is what kind of degree you need to achieve it. With pharmacy, there are a few different ways to go about getting the qualifications you need.

Education Paths

Anyone who wishes to become a pharmacist must acquire a Doctor of Pharmacy Degree (PharmD.) from an Accreditation Council for Pharmacy Education accredited program. There are a few ways you can go about this:

  1. 6 year program: This is a PharmD program that includes undergraduate coursework as well as professional pharmacy studies, allowing you to leave the program with a PharmD.

  2. 4 year program: This is a purely professional pharmacy education program that does not include undergraduate studies.

  3. 0-6 or Early Assurance: Those who decide while they are still in High School that they want to pursue a pharmacy degree may be able to apply for an "0-6" or "early assurance" program, which offers guaranteed admission into a pharmacy school after you have completed at least two years of undergraduate coursework in relevant areas.

  4. Late Decision: Those who decide during or after college that they wish to pursue a PharmD are still able to apply for a 4 year pharmacy program as long as they have the relevant prerequisite undergraduate experience over at least two years.


Some undergraduate courses include: biology, chemistry (general and organic, physics, english, math, and economics. Professional level courses are more specific.

I Graduated; What Now?

The certifications you need to be a licensed pharmacist vary by jurisdiction, however one exam you will have to pass no matter what is the North American Pharmacist Licensure Exam, or NAPLEX.

You also will have to take an exam on pharmacy law, a common one being the Multistate Pharmacy Jurisprudence Exam (MPJE). Different states, however, will have different exams you are required to pass. These two are guaranteed, however, state-by-state there may be additional tests you are required to pass to earn your license.

You also need a certain number of practical experience hours (internships), however most people complete these while they are still in school

Lastly, you will need to submit to a criminal background check (if it is required in your jurisdiction).



Congrats! You are now a licensed pharmacist, and you are free to practice pharmacy.
